You can love your child with all your heart and still be exhausted.

You love them. God knows you do.

You’d sell everything, fight anybody, stay up all night for them.



You’ve memorized therapy schedules. You know which school will actually take your child. You’ve explained "he’s not being rude, he has autism" to 5 strangers this week alone.




And you are tired. Bone-deep tired.




But you can’t say it.

Because what kind of mother says "I’m tired of this"?

What kind of father admits "I miss my life before"?




So you post "God is good" on Sunday.

Then you cry in the car for 10 minutes before driving home.




If that’s you, breathe. You’re not evil. You’re not ungrateful.

You’re human. And you deserve someone to say: "I see you. This is hard."




## **What This Kind of Tired Actually Feels Like**

It’s not just "I didn’t sleep well". It’s different.




#### 1. The Exhaustion That Doesn’t Go Away

Therapy Tuesday. Hospital Thursday. Meltdown at 2am. IEP meeting Friday.

Your body is running on pure adrenaline and "God help me".




## 2. The Quiet Grief

You grieve the birthday parties you imagined.

The "mummy, I got first in class" you thought you’d hear.

Then you feel guilty for grieving. Because your child is here. They’re alive.

Both things are true and it’s confusing.




## 3. The Money Wahala

Let’s be real. 1 therapy session in Lagos/Ibadan/Abuja can be quite expensive.

Special school fees. Drugs. Special diet. Transport. [That money anxiety](https://mindmed.com.ng/blog/money-anxiety-nigeria/) is real.

At night you lie awake thinking: "What happens to them if something happens to me?"



#### 4. Your Marriage Feels Like Shift Work

One person is always "on".

You’re roommates managing a medical case.

Date night? What’s that. Sex? You’re too tired. Talking? You only talk about the child.




#### 5. The Loneliness

Friends: "Let’s hang out"

You: "I can’t, we have therapy"

After 3 times they stop asking.

Family: "Just pray and have faith"

So you stop talking. You put on the "Smiling Parent" mask.




If you’re nodding at 2 or more of these, that’s caregiver burnout. And it’s valid.




## **Why It Hits Different in Nigeria**

Other countries have respite care. We have "aunty, please watch him for me".




#### 1. The Stigma is Loud

"Is it witchcraft?"

"What did you do during pregnancy?"

"Have you taken him to the prayer mountain?"

You get advice instead of help.




#### 2. No Breaks

There’s nowhere to drop your child for 3 hours so you can sleep.

Most schools say "we don’t take that kind". The ones that do charge ₦1.5M per term.




#### 3. You Become 5 People

You’re the nurse, therapist, teacher, advocate, and parent.

You’re on Google at 1am: "how to handle autistic meltdown" "ADHD schools in Ibadan".

It’s too much.




#### 4. "Be Strong"

"God knows why He gave you this child."

So you carry it. You don’t complain. Until your body starts breaking down.




## **Things You’re Probably Thinking But Won’t Say**

Let’s say them here. I’ll start:

"I’m angry. At the situation. At God. At everyone."

"I miss my old life."

"Sometimes I wish I could just... not for one weekend."

"I love my child. I hate this."

"I’m terrified of their future."




Writing that down didn’t make you a bad parent.

It made you honest. And honest parents get help.




## **How To Keep Going Without Breaking**

You can’t pour from an empty cup. Your child needs a whole you.




## 1. Get Therapy. For YOU.

Not for your child. For you.

To cry. To be angry. To not have to be strong.

\[MindMed has mental health practitioners](/book) who understand caregiver stress. You can do it from home after they sleep. 50 minutes that are just yours.




## 2. Find Your People

Facebook groups like "Nigerian Parents of Children with Autism" or "Down Syndrome Parents Nigeria" are lifesavers.

No explaining. Just "today was hard" and 20 people say "me too".




#### 3. Ask And Accept Help.

"Can you sit with her while I shower?"

"Can you drop him at therapy on Thursday?"

People mean well. Let them show up. Rest is not laziness.




#### 4. Hold Two Truths

"I love my child" AND "This is really hard".

When you stop fighting the second one, the guilt gets lighter.




5.Count the Small Wins

He said a new word. She ate without a fight. You made it through the week.

That’s not small. That’s a miracle.

Your child needs you.

But they need a well you.

What you’re doing is one of the hardest jobs in the world.
