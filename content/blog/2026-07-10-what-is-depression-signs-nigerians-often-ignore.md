You wake up. Your body feels heavy, like you slept with a bag of cement on your chest. You brush your teeth, go to work, laugh at your colleague’s joke. But inside, everything feels gray. 

What if depression doesn’t look like crying like the movies made it? 

What if it looks like sleeping 12 hours and still feeling tired? Snapping at the okada man over a meager change? Losing interest in your favorite food or activities? 

In Nigeria we call it by different labels, but not what it actually is — Depression. And it wears different disguises. Here are 7 signs we ignore because they don’t look like our expectations of depression.

## What Depression Actually Is

Depression is not just “feeling sad”. It’s a medical condition. Your chemicals in your brain that are in charge of your moods— serotonin and dopamine — drop low for weeks or months. Think of it like a fever, but for your mood instead of your body temperature.

WHO estimates **over 20 million Nigerians** experience depression every year. Market women in Onitsha. Students in Zaria. Bankers in Abuja. Pastors in Port Harcourt. It’s not a “white people problem”.

And the most important thing: **Depression ≠ laziness.** You can’t “snap out of it” the same way you can’t snap out of malaria. Your brain needs help, just like your body would.

## 7 Signs Nigerians Ignore — Because They Don’t Look Like ‘Sadness’

### The Heavy Body Sign

You sleep 10 hours but wake up drained. Your legs feel like you walked from Ikeja to Marina. Even brushing your teeth feels like work. People say “you’re just lazy”. But doctors call this “psychomotor fatigue”. Depression steals your body’s energy before it reaches your mind. If rest doesn’t fix your tiredness for weeks, pay attention.

### The Numbness Sign

Your favorite food tastes like cardboard. Your team wins the and you go “nice”. Salary drops and you feel nothing. Nigerians call this “hard heart”. Therapists call it anhedonia. It is losing interest in things that used to bring joy. This is one of depression’s most common disguises.

### The Anger Mask Sign

NEPA takes light and you’re cursing for 20 minutes. Your child spills water and you yell. A customer asks one question and you snap. Especially common in Nigerian men. They’ve been taught “men don’t cry”, so depression shows up as anger instead. If small things trigger big rage and you don’t know why, your brain might be signaling distress.

### The “I’m Fine” Isolation Sign

You stop replying WhatsApp chats. Skip church on Sundays. Avoid family meetings. Skip out on your favorite people owanbe. Not because you hate people. Because pretending to be “okay” is exhausting. So you disappear quietly. Isolation feels safe, but it makes depression louder.

### The Guilt Loop Sign

A mistake from several years ago keeps replaying in your head. “I disappointed my parents.” “God is angry with me.” “I should have done better.” Your brain becomes judge, jury, and executioner. Normal people feel guilt for a day. Depression makes guilt live in your head rent-free for months. It’s a symptom.

### The Body Pain Sign

You have a headache that won’t go. Back pain. Stomach issues. You go to the hospital, run tests, doctor says “everything is normal”. But the pain is real. Your body is speaking what your mouth won’t say: “I’m not okay.” In Nigeria we treat body pain faster than emotional pain. But sometimes the body serves as the messenger for the mind.

### The Quiet Thoughts Sign

You don't have a full suicide plan, not yet. Just quiet thoughts that visit at 3am: “What if I wasn’t here?” “Maybe everyone will rest.” “It would be easier if I disappeared.” Even 3 seconds of this counts. It doesn’t mean you’re weak or faithless. It means your brain is sick and needs help, fast.

## 3 Gentle Next Steps If This Feels Like You

#### Stop fighting yourself

Say it out loud: “I might be depressed.” Naming it removes shame. You’re not failing God or your family. Your brain chemistry is low, like low blood sugar. And it can be treated.

#### Tell one human

Not an AI or a post on your WhatsApp status. One person you trust. Family, friends, pastor, colleagues. Say “I’ve been feeling off lately.” You don’t need solutions from them. You just need to not carry it alone. Depression grows in silence and dies in conversation.

## If This Is Someone You Love

You won’t fix them with one speech or conversation. Don’t say “man up” or “it’s the devil”. Try: “I don’t understand what you’re feeling, but I believe you. I’m not going anywhere.” Stay, listen, send food. Sit in silence with them. Your presence is a treatment.
