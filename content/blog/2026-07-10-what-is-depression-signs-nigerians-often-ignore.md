You wake up. Your body feels heavy, like you slept with a bag of cement on your chest. You brush your teeth, go to work, laugh at your colleague’s joke. But inside, everything feels gray. 




What if depression doesn’t look like crying like the movies made it? 

What if it looks like sleeping 12 hours and still feeling tired? Snapping at the okada man over a meager change? Losing interest in your favorite food or activities? 




In Nigeria we call it by different labels, but not what it actually is — Depression. And it wears different disguises. Here are 7 signs we ignore because they don’t look like our expectations of depression.



What Depression Actually Is




Depression is not just “feeling sad”. It’s a medical condition. Your chemicals in your brain that are in charge of your moods— serotonin and dopamine — drop low for weeks or months. Think of it like a fever, but for your mood instead of your body temperature.




WHO estimates **over 20 million Nigerians** experience depression every year. Market women in Onitsha. Students in Zaria. Bankers in Abuja. Pastors in Port Harcourt. It’s not a “white people problem”.




And the most important thing: **Depression ≠ laziness.** You can’t “snap out of it” the same way you can’t snap out of malaria. Your brain needs help, just like your body would.
