If at any point you’ve heard “just pray about it” when talking about **anxiety**, or “that’s just village people” when you’re going through **depression**, you’ve felt stigma. Stigma stops Nigerians from getting the **help they deserve** while struggling with **mental health issues.**




Most times, these stigmas come as jokes, advice, or even sermons.




Today, let's  break 7 myths and swap them for facts you can share with friends and family.




What is Mental Health Stigma in Nigeria?

**Mental health stigma** is the negative stereotypes, attitudes, and behaviors directed toward people living with **mental health conditions**. In Nigeria, words like **“madness,” “spiritual attack,” “village people,”** and “weak faith” are used to dismiss people struggling.




There are real cases: people with **bipolar disorder** taken to churches to “expel demons,” elderly people with **dementia** shamed as witches, and children with **developmental disorders** bullied in schools. This needs to stop.




In many Nigerian homes, mental health is still a taboo topic. Parents tell the children, “stop acting funny”, instead of asking if they are okay. This silence creates shame and shame doesn't heal anything.




7 Myths vs Facts About Mental Health

Myth 1: Mental illness = spiritual attack 

**Why Nigerians still believe this:** For generations, Africans explained illness through spirituality. When science was not accessible, ‘spiritual attack’ made sense.




**Fact:** **Depression and anxiety** are **medical conditions**, not curses. The brain produces chemicals like **serotonin**, and when levels drop, mood is affected. Prayer gives hope, therapy gives tools — they work together. Many pastors and imams in Nigeria now recommend **therapy and counseling** alongside faith.

Myth 2: Only “**rich people**” get therapy

**Fact:** Therapy is accessible to all, not just the upper class. Free helplines, university counseling centers, and low-cost NGOs exist across Nigeria.

Myth 3: Talking about feelings is weakness

**Fact:** Talking reduces stress. Strong people ask for help. You don’t have to carry every burden alone.




According to the WHO 2023 data, over **20 million Nigerians** live with mental health conditions, yet less than **10%** get professional help. The gap exists because of the stigma related to the condition, not because of the lack of help.

Myth 4: Therapy is only for “mad people”

**Fact**: Students use therapy for exam stress. Workers use it for **burnout**. Moms use it for **postpartum stress**. You’re not “mad” — you’re human, and therapy gives you tools + clarity.

Myth 5: Medication makes you a “zombie”

**Fact**: Prescribed meds are monitored by doctors and **psychiatrists**. Not everyone needs medication. If you’re prescribed any, follow your doctor’s instructions and never self-medicate.

Myth 6: Men don’t get depressed

Why men stay silent: Nigerian culture teaches boys to hide their pain. From childhood to adulthood, an average Nigerian man has been told more than once that “boys/men don't cry”.




**Fact**: 1 in 5 Nigerian men face depression but stay silent to avoid being called “weak.” Being a “hard guy” shouldn’t cost you your life.

Myth 7: You should “just be strong”

**Fact**: Real strength is knowing when to get support. Showing strength means choosing to heal.




Why Stigma is Dangerous in Nigeria

1. **People suffer in silence:** Fear of being ostracized, pitied, or judged keeps people from speaking up.

2. **Suicide rates rise:** When people can’t talk, hopelessness grows. Stigma directly contributes to higher suicide rates nationwide.

3. **Families break:** Lack of communication + untreated conditions strain marriages, parent-child bonds, and friendships.




How You Can Fight Stigma Today

**5 simple actions:**

1. Learn the facts about mental health

2. Speak up gently when you hear harmful myths  

3. Share articles like this with one person

4. Support friends who open up to you

5. Use and share helpline numbers




You don't have to **fight stigma** alone. Every share, every conversation, every ‘are you okay?’ breaks the silence a little more.
